On "should have"s and "I can't"s

03 February 2016 on non-tech

Tomorrow is my last day in San Francisco, California for the foreseeable future. As I am about the leave greatest city on the earth after 5.5 years my mind unsurprisingly wanders among "should have", "could have" and "would have"s.

I should have;

"went out more", "dated more", "been more daring", "learned surfing", "visited Tahoe" (in my defense there was not single snow flake in Tahoe for the first 4 years), "attended more meetups", "read more", "written more", "spent more time in different neighborhoods", "worked out more", "appreciated the city more", list goes on...

I realize that once I started some of these phrases with "I can't". "I can't surf", "I can't go to a blind date", etc. "Can't" is deterministic. "Can't" kills the cat before you open the box and steals the experiment from you. Even though failing and not starting by saying "can't" might appear same from a pragmatic viewpoint, the loss of experience can not be underestimated. There is value in putting yourself out, battling and even in failing. The road is usually more interesting than the destination.

Here's to another great city, Istanbul, to less "should have"s and more "glad I tried"s!

I don't want to close this great chapter of my life on a sad note so here is the good stuff.

San Francisco taught me

"that I am good at building things", "building things give me feelings of euphoria", "avocado, bacon and sushi are great", "coincidences make life great", "spending every dime I own on travels is a good idea", "choosing the weirdest thing on a menu is a risk worth taking", "you depart ways with people and it is OK", "that I am a day person and nothing beats a sunny afternoon in a beer garden", "seeing a movie alone is not that bad" and "if everything goes wrong I will always have San Francisco to go back".

Thank you San Francisco!
